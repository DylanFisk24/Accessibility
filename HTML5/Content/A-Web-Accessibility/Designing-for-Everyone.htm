Designing for Everyone

Accessibility is certainly important in making web content available for disabled individuals. But accessible content can actually make things better for everyone. By considering design with this larger perspective, accessibility can become a method through which the whole design process flows, improving your content at large.

The "Curb-Cut Effect"

The “curb-cut effect” is the phenomenon in which implementing features meant to help those with disabilities ends up positively impacting everyone. The phrase comes from the introduction of curb-cuts into American streets in the 1970s. What was initially an act of defiance by a few disabled individuals in Berkeley, CA in pursuit of accessible infrastructure quickly became an architectural staple on almost every US street. These curb-cuts not only benefit wheelchair users but also “parents pushing strollers,” “workers pushing heavy carts,” and “travelers wheeling luggage”1 Blackwell, Angela Glover. “The Curb-Cut Effect (SSIR).” The Curb-Cut Effect, Stanford Social Innovation Review, Winter 2017, ssir.org/articles/entry/the_curb_cut_effect.. The robust usefulness of curb-cuts for all kinds of people encourages us to think about accessibility as a universal pursuit beyond its utility as a vehicle of equality and justice for those with disabilities.

Accessible Design vs. Universal Design

When implementing accessibility, two common approaches are used:

  1. Accessible design – simply "considers the needs of people with disabilities."

  2. Universal design – considers the usability of “all people, to the greatest extent possible, without the need for adaptation or specialized design”2 Kalbag, Laura. “Considering Accessibility.” Accessibility for Everyone, A Book Apart, 2017..

While accessible design may be functional, it can often lead to last-minute add-ons and patronizing solutions where the designer assumes they know what is best for those with disabilities. This approach can lead to design that is incompatible with the real tools and experiences employed by its target users and that further isolate the individuals who need to use these “add-ons.”

Universal design, on the other hand, treats accessibility as a natural part of the design process. Content designed with this approach prioritizes the ability for the widest variety of individuals to use it. Such an approach incorporates accessibility features into the design itself, considering the needs of all of its users from the ground up. Done well, universal design takes the experiences of all kinds of real users, including those with disabilities, and uses them to create the most comprehensive user experience possible. As content designers, we should strive for universal design whenever possible, as it not only provides the most seamless accessibility features, but it serves to extend the reach of our content to the most people possible.
