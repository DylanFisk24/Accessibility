About This Project

For my final project in English 381: Advanced Tech Writing, I put together this website using MadCap Flare featuring information on web accessibility with a focus on making accessible video content. The website offers an in-depth analysis of web accessibility and practical guidance on implementing accessibility features when developing video content.

Objectives

  • Demonstrate my understanding of web accessibility, a crucial topic for any web developer or content creator, to potential employers

  • Showcase my ability to utilize MadCap Flare to create web content, increasing the tools I am proficient with

About Me

I am Dylan Fisk, a passionate writer and aspiring web content creator/tech writer. I am expected to graduate in May 2026 with a B.A. of English from the University of Massachusetts Amherst with a Creative Writing Concentration and a Professional Writing and Technical Communication Certificate. Through my coursework, I have developed a portfolio website using HTML, created a 25-page manual documenting Microsoft Word, and put together a poetry portfolio, among other papers and projects. When I am not developing web content, I enjoy tutoring at my university's Writing Center and playing video games.

My Role + Collaboration

The content for this project was split into two parts: web accessibility content and video accessibility content.

I wrote and revised all of the web accessibility content myself, drawing on course readings and discussions, as well as brief independent research to gather the information. My professor, Janine Solberg, offered some feedback as well, which I incorporated into my content revisions.

For the video accessibility content, my class was divided into six groups, with each group focused on one specific aspect of video accessibility (group divisions are reflected by the different Video Accessibility dropdown menus in the website's side navigation). I worked alongside Jane Edison and Zoe Rakarich to draft the Closed Captions section, and I was personally responsible for drafting the step-by-step instructional pages on adding and editing closed captions in Clipchamp. All of the groups drafted their content on a shared Google Doc, which we as a class collaboratively distributed feedback on.

Once all of the content had been revised, I transferred every group's content into my project using MadCap Flare. I made some small edits during the transfer process to make sure all of the formatting and citations were consistent across the different groups' sections, but other than that, the content belongs entirely to the group members who created them (group members' names and AI statements can be found at the bottom of each webpage).

Tools

In the making of this project, I utilized MadCap Flare's XML and HTML Text Editor tools to put together the topic pages. My class and I documented Clipchamp to create the step-by-step instructional pages of the project. I also used Microsoft Word to draft my individual web accessibility content and a shared Google Doc to collaborate on our group content.

Workflow

When taking on a project of this scope, I found it helpful to split it up into smaller chunks. Much of this chunking was built into the course, like the splitting of the individual content and the group content, which helped me focus my attention in the right place at the right time. I also found it helpful that we drafted our content outside of MadCap Flare before transferring everything into the program. This allowed us to determine how we wanted to split up the content and what we wanted it to look like before finalizing it in Flare, which certainly saved me time in the end.

For the actual process of transferring, I was able to fall into the rhythm of adding content to MadCap Flare through the constant repetition of creating content pages, formatting them semantically, adding images, and placing them in the right order in the PDF and Online Table of Contents. The more progress I made on the transfer process, the more confidently and efficiently I was able to transfer the subsequent content. I also found it helpful to have decided on a consistent format for each of the groups' sections, since it made my editing work straightforward.

Top 3 Takeaways

Working on this project has helped me develop a better sense of my work as a technical writer and a better understanding of what kinds of tools are suitable for what tasks. A few key takeaways include:

  1. Web accessibility is a critical consideration for anyone who designs web content. It is best executed when it is considered from the beginning of the creation process in the mindset of universal design, since it leads to more user-friendly design overall that will have a positive impact on any user.

  2. As a technical writer, I enjoy exploring software and documenting processes in user-friendly ways. I could see myself pursuing a technical writing career despite my initial hesitancy.

  3. Although MadCap Flare has its shortcomings, I can see how it works to save developers time with its Microsoft Word-adjacent layout and ability to output as HTML5 and PDF files. Overall, I feel confident using it to accomplish both simple and advanced web design tasks. I also feel confident in my ability to learn a similar Help Authoring Tool (HAT) if it becomes a part of my job in the future.
